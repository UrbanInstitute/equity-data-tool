Spatial Equity Data Tool

Measuring Disparities in Your City

How can cities ensure that resources are equitably distributed to all residents? We created this data tool to help city officials, community organizations, and residents quickly assess spatial and demographic disparities in their cities.

Are wi-fi hotspots unevenly located in your city? Do all residents have equitable access to bike share stations? Does your dataset accurately reflect your city’s population? What groups or neighborhoods are underrepresented?

This tool can reveal biases in the datasets policymakers use to make decisions or illustrate disparities in how resources are distributed throughout the city. Check out our sample datasets or upload your own data.

CASE STUDY

This tool uses data from the five-year (2014–18) American Community Survey to assess spatial and demographic disparities in your data. Once a user uploads data, the tool geocodes the dataset to a US city and compares the distribution of the uploaded data with the distribution of baseline variables from the ACS. For more information about the specific methodology and variables used, please see the technical appendix.

For the demographic disparity chart, we use “Latinx” to refer to people of Latino or Hispanic origin as asked by the Census. We recognize that this gender-neutral term may not be the preferred identifier for many, but we use it to remain inclusive of gender non-conforming and non-binary people. We use White, Black, and Asian to refer to non-Hispanic White, Black, and Asian populations.

This tool is part of a larger project to help cities more equitably use data and technology. Through 40 interviews and a roundtable with city leaders, we identified key questions cities should ask to center equity in technology programs and best practices to answer those questions, which we have documented in Creating Equitable Technology Programs: A Guide for Cities. Our research identified the need for resources to facilitate regular and rigorous examination of the representativeness of data powering city decisionmaking and the distribution of technology, and other, programs. That need was a driving force behind this tool.

For more information about our sample datasets, see our entry in Urban Institute’s Data Catalog.

For help using this tool or for guidance designing and implementing equitable technology programs, contact anarayanan@urban.org.
